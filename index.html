The Commodity

Salt

An essential mineral we cannot live without, it can be obtained in a variety of ways — some more harmful than others.

It is required for human and animal health — to support the nervous system and hydration retention.

It is used to preserve food and extend shelf-life through non-growing seasons.

It is a primary component of chemical manufacturing, water treatment, and weather-precaution treatment.

The Human Dimension

The Agariyas

"They work in conditions of extreme heat, standing ankle-deep in brine for hours. The salt corrodes their skin. The sun blinds. And yet — they produce 30% of Gujarat's entire output."

Agariyas are traditionally nomadic communities who migrate seasonally to the salt flats. Despite fueling India's dominant global position, they remain invisible in supply chains and ESG frameworks.

Research Synthesis

Key Findings

01
Türkiye as Global Fulcrum

Simultaneous supply to the US (10.2%), China (6.1%), and India (4.2%) positions Türkiye as a uniquely multilateral pivot in global mineral trade — a geopolitical asset rarely examined.

02
India's Production Paradox

India produces 30M tons yet imports $2.06B from 103 countries. Gujarat's production miracle conceals structural dependencies in downstream value chains.

03
China–Australia Entanglement

Australia supplies 34.7% of China's HS25 imports despite diplomatic tensions. This dependency is both a strategic vulnerability and a bilateral negotiating lever.

04
Labor Invisibility

The Agariyas produce 30% of Gujarat's salt — $600M+ contribution — yet remain excluded from supply-chain transparency, ESG reporting, and fair compensation globally.
